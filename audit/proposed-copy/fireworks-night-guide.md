## Title
**Your Fireworks Night Game Plan: how to help an anxious dog cope**

## Intro
If your dog shakes, hides or paces through fireworks, you're not doing anything wrong — and there's
a lot you can do to make the night easier. Here's a calm, practical plan you can set up in advance.
None of this "cures" noise phobia (for severe cases, please talk to your vet or a behaviourist) —
but done together, these steps genuinely take the edge off.

## The plan (set up *before* dusk)
1. **Build a den before dark.** Dogs cope better with somewhere covered to hide. A cave bed in a
   quiet, curtained room, blankets over a crate — give them a corner that's theirs. *(→ Deep Nook)*
2. **Muffle the world.** Close windows and curtains, put on the TV or calm music to cover the bangs.
   A snood can take the edge off the sharpest noise for some dogs. *(→ Calming Snood)*
3. **Gentle pressure helps many dogs.** A snug calming coat gives constant, reassuring pressure —
   like a hug — and a lot of owners find it settles the shaking. Put it on *before* they're
   frightened, not mid-panic. *(→ Calming Coat)*
4. **Give the nose a job.** A loaded snuffle mat, lick mat or snuffle ball gives an anxious dog
   something repetitive and self-soothing to do. Sniffing and licking lower arousal. *(→ enrichment)*
5. **Walk early, feed early.** Big walk in daylight before any fireworks start; feed before dusk so
   a nervous tummy isn't dealing with dinner too.
6. **Stay normal and nearby.** Don't punish the fear, and you can absolutely comfort them — the old
   "don't reassure them" advice is outdated. Calm, boring, present. That's the job.
7. **Secure the exits.** Most dogs bolt on firework night. Doors and gates shut, ID tag and microchip
   details up to date, before it gets dark.

## What *not* to do
- Don't force them out for a late walk once fireworks start.
- Don't shut them somewhere they can't get to their safe den.
- Don't reach for anything medical without your vet — no human calming products, ever.

## The honest bit
Comfort kit helps a dog *cope*; it doesn't switch off fear. If your dog's noise phobia is severe or
getting worse each year, that's a vet/behaviourist conversation — desensitisation programmes work,
and your vet can advise on whether anything else is appropriate. We'll never sell you a "cure",
because there isn't one in a box.
